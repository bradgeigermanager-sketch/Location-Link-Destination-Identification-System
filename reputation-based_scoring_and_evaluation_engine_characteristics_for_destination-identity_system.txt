In the reputation-based destination system we've been discussing, a **reputation engine** detects anomalies by comparing current behavior against expected behavior. The key idea is:

> Trust isn't based only on what a destination claims to be, but on whether it behaves like it has historically behaved.

### 1. Baseline Modeling

The engine maintains a profile for each destination.

```text
Earth Port 01

Marker: A417
Sponsors: 4
Route Changes: 0/year
Audit Failures: 0
Arrivals: 99.999%
```

This becomes the destination's expected pattern.

When new observations arrive, they are compared against the baseline.

***

### 2. Change Detection

The simplest anomaly detector looks for differences.

```text
Yesterday:
Earth → Marker A417

Today:
Earth → Marker B923
```

Even if Marker B923 is valid, the change itself is unusual.

The engine asks:

* Was this approved?
* Does it match historical behavior?
* Did all sponsors authorize it?

***

### 3. Rate-of-Change Monitoring

A healthy destination might rarely change.

```text
10 years:
0 reassignments
```

Then suddenly:

```text
3 reassignments
in 24 hours
```

The destination may still be operational, but the behavior is statistically abnormal.

***

### 4. Relationship Analysis

The engine looks at connected entities.

```text
Destination
├── Marker
├── Sponsors
├── Neighboring Locations
└── Links
```

Suppose:

```text
Marker unchanged
Sponsors unchanged
Links changed
```

That inconsistency may indicate a hidden routing modification.

Anomaly detection becomes more powerful when examining relationships rather than isolated objects.

***

### 5. Reputation Drift Detection

Reputation usually changes gradually.

Normal:

```text
95
94
95
96
95
```

Suspicious:

```text
95
95
96
52
95
```

Large jumps can indicate:

* a compromised authority,
* corrupted records,
* unauthorized changes,
* or sensor failures.

***

### 6. Consensus Anomalies

Multiple authorities report independent observations.

```text
Transit Authority:
Earth

Security Authority:
Earth

Audit Authority:
Mars
```

The disagreement itself becomes the anomaly.

Even if nobody knows which record is correct, the lack of consensus requires investigation.

***

### 7. Lineage Analysis

The engine tracks destination history.

```text
Earth Port
    ↓
Marker A417
20 Years
```

If a new marker suddenly inherits the reputation:

```text
Earth Port
    ↓
Marker B923
1 Day
```

the engine notices a mismatch between identity age and reputation age.

This is particularly useful against the "marker switching" scenario you've been exploring.

***

### 8. Traffic Pattern Analysis

The system can observe actual usage.

Normal:

```text
100,000 arrivals
100,000 departures
```

Abnormal:

```text
100,000 departures
40,000 arrivals
```

or

```text
Earth traffic
suddenly resembles
Mars traffic
```

Such shifts suggest a routing problem even if identity records appear valid.

***

### 9. Cross-Location Validation

Locations verify each other.

```text
Earth says:
Linked to Mars

Mars says:
Linked to Earth
```

Expected.

But:

```text
Earth says:
Linked to Mars

Mars says:
Linked to Station X
```

creates an anomaly.

The reputation engine treats conflicting reports as evidence requiring review.

***

### 10. Composite Risk Scoring

Most sophisticated systems would combine many signals.

```text
Risk =
Identity Change
+ Sponsor Disagreement
+ Reputation Drift
+ Routing Variance
+ Audit History
```

For example:

```text
Earth Port

Marker Changed: Yes
Sponsor Approval: Missing
Traffic Pattern: Abnormal
Consensus: Broken

Risk Score: 92/100
```

Automatic investigation begins.

***

### Most Advanced Form

The most advanced reputation engine wouldn't merely ask:

> "Did a marker change?"

It would ask:

> "Does the entire behavior of this destination still match the identity it claims to have?"

It evaluates:

* historical behavior,
* marker consistency,
* link consistency,
* sponsor relationships,
* authority consensus,
* traffic patterns,
* audit results.

In a destination-identity network, that's what makes reputation engines valuable: they detect situations where every individual component appears valid, yet the overall pattern suggests that something—such as an unauthorized link reassignment or marker switch—has occurred.
