In a destination-identity system, the best approach is usually **not** to let any single anomaly trigger the final decision. Instead, multiple signals are combined into a **risk score** or **confidence score**.

## Layer 1: Assign Signal Severity

Each anomaly contributes points.

For example:

```text
Marker Identity Change          +50
Missing Authorization           +40
Sponsor Disagreement            +30
Link Reassignment               +25
Traffic Anomaly                 +10
Audit Warning                   +10
```

Then:

```text
Total Risk =
50 + 40 + 30 = 120
```

Higher scores indicate greater concern.

***

## Layer 2: Weight by Reliability

Some signals are more trustworthy than others.

Example:

```text
Physical Marker Verification
Weight: 1.0

Authority Consensus
Weight: 0.9

Traffic Analysis
Weight: 0.5

User Reports
Weight: 0.2
```

Then:

```text
Risk Contribution =
Severity × Weight
```

A confirmed marker mismatch should carry more weight than an unusual traffic pattern.

***

## Layer 3: Correlation Boosting

Multiple related anomalies occurring together should increase concern faster than treating them independently.

For example:

```text
Marker Change
+
Sponsor Disagreement
+
Link Change
```

may indicate a common root cause.

Instead of:

```text
50 + 30 + 25 = 105
```

the system adds a correlation factor:

```text
105 + 30 = 135
```

because these events reinforce one another.

***

## Layer 4: Time-Based Aggregation

Recent signals matter more.

Example:

```text
Marker Change:
1 hour ago

Sponsor Disagreement:
30 minutes ago

Traffic Anomaly:
10 minutes ago
```

The clustering itself is suspicious.

A common model is:

```text
Effective Risk =
Risk × Time Weight
```

where newer events receive higher weight.

***

## Layer 5: Destination Reputation Adjustment

The same anomaly can mean different things depending on the destination.

For example:

```text
Destination Reputation: 99
```

may receive some benefit of the doubt.

Whereas:

```text
Destination Reputation: 60
```

may trigger investigation immediately.

A simple model:

```text
Adjusted Risk =
Raw Risk ÷ Reputation Factor
```

or

```text
Adjusted Risk =
Raw Risk × Trust Modifier
```

***

## Layer 6: Multi-Authority Consensus

You can also combine authority opinions.

Example:

```text
Transit Authority:
Risk 80

Security Authority:
Risk 85

Audit Authority:
Risk 90
```

Combined:

```text
Consensus Risk:
85
```

If one authority reports:

```text
20
```

while others report:

```text
90
```

the disagreement becomes an anomaly itself.

***

## Layer 7: Confidence Scores

An advanced system tracks confidence separately from risk.

Example:

```text
Risk: 85
Confidence: High
```

versus

```text
Risk: 85
Confidence: Low
```

The first comes from strong evidence.

The second comes from weak or incomplete evidence.

This helps distinguish:

```text
Likely Compromise
```

from

```text
Possible Compromise
```

***

## Example: Marker-Switching Scenario

Suppose the system observes:

```text
Marker Changed                +50
Missing Approval              +40
Sponsor Disagreement          +30
Traffic Shift                 +10
```

Base score:

```text
130
```

Correlation bonus:

```text
+20
```

Recent-event multiplier:

```text
×1.2
```

Final score:

```text
180
```

Thresholds:

```text
0-30     Normal
31-60    Warning
61-100   Investigation
101-150  High Risk
151+      Critical
```

Result:

```text
Critical
```

Automatic escalation occurs.

***

A mature system would therefore combine anomaly signals using:

```text
Weighted Signals
      +
Correlation Analysis
      +
Time Analysis
      +
Authority Consensus
      +
Reputation Context
      +
Confidence Estimation
      =
Final Risk Score
```

This prevents overreacting to a single odd event while still detecting complex situations where several small anomalies together indicate an unauthorized destination or marker change.
