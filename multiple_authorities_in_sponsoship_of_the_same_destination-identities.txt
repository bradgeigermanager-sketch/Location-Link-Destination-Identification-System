A mature destination-identity system would likely **prefer multiple authorities sponsoring a destination**, because it reduces the risk that a single compromised authority could introduce an unauthorized marker or destination.

### Simple Model

Instead of:

```text
Earth Transit Authority
           ↓
      Destination
```

you have:

```text
Transit Authority
        +
Security Authority
        +
Regional Authority
        ↓
    Destination
```

The destination gains trust only when all required sponsors agree.

***

### Why Multiple Sponsors Help

Each authority can validate a different aspect of the destination.

```text
Transit Authority
→ Routing validity

Security Authority
→ Marker integrity

Regional Authority
→ Physical existence

Port Authority
→ Operational status
```

A destination's identity becomes a combination of independent attestations.

***

### Reputation Weighting

Sponsors need not contribute equally.

For example:

```text
Transit Authority
Reputation: 98
Weight: 40%

Security Authority
Reputation: 95
Weight: 40%

Regional Authority
Reputation: 90
Weight: 20%
```

The destination's initial trust could be derived from the weighted average of its sponsors.

***

### Consensus-Based Approval

More sophisticated systems might require a quorum.

Examples:

```text
Any 2 of 3 sponsors
```

or

```text
3 of 5 sponsors
```

or even

```text
All sponsors must agree
```

before a marker reassignment becomes active.

This makes "three-card monte" style switching much harder, because an attacker would need to compromise multiple authorities simultaneously.

***

### Cross-Monitoring

Multiple sponsors can also monitor one another.

```text
Authority A:
Earth Marker A

Authority B:
Earth Marker A

Authority C:
Earth Marker B
```

The disagreement itself becomes an alert.

In this model, security doesn't depend on everyone being honest—it depends on at least one independent authority noticing something wrong.

***

### Sponsor Graphs

The most advanced version becomes a trust network:

```text
Destination
├── Transit Authority
├── Security Authority
├── Customs Authority
├── Regional Authority
└── Infrastructure Authority
```

Each sponsor contributes evidence.

If one sponsor withdraws support:

```text
4 sponsors active
Score = 97

3 sponsors active
Score = 88

1 sponsor active
Score = 55
```

The destination remains usable but becomes progressively less trusted.

***

### Handling Marker Changes

In the marker-switching scenario you've been exploring, a reassignment might require:

```text
Current Marker:
A417

Proposed Marker:
B923

Required Approvals:
✓ Transit Authority
✓ Security Authority
✓ Regional Authority
✓ Audit Authority
```

Only after all approvals are recorded does the destination inherit the historical reputation associated with the old marker.

Without those approvals, the system would treat the new marker as an unsponsored destination with little or no reputation, making unauthorized substitutions immediately suspicious.

So in a mature destination-identity architecture, **multiple authorities sponsoring a single destination is not merely possible—it is likely the preferred security model**, because trust becomes distributed, auditable, and substantially harder to manipulate.
