In the **Location–Link–Destination Identification System**, a **reputation-based identity** system would answer a different question than ordinary authentication.

Authentication asks:

> "Is this destination really Earth Port 01?"

Reputation asks:

> "Has Earth Port 01 behaved consistently enough over time that we trust it?"

## Basic Idea

Instead of trusting a destination solely because it presents the correct identity, the network maintains a historical trust record.

```text
Destination
    ↓
Identity Verification
    ↓
Behavior History
    ↓
Trust Score
    ↓
Routing Decision
```

Every destination accumulates a history.

For example:

```text
Earth Port 01

Identity Matches: 100%
Successful Arrivals: 99.999%
Unexpected Changes: 0
Inspections Passed: 487
Trust Score: 99.98
```

## Why Authentication Isn't Enough

Imagine a marker switch occurs.

The attacker manages to modify the system so that:

```text
Earth Port 01
     ↓
New Marker
```

is now considered officially valid.

Identity checks alone might not catch it immediately.

A reputation system would notice sudden changes:

```text
Historical Marker:
A417

Current Marker:
B923
```

and ask:

> Why did a destination with 50 years of stable behavior suddenly change identity?

The destination becomes suspicious even if technical authentication appears valid.

## Reputation Components

A sophisticated system might calculate trust from many sources.

### Stability

Has the destination remained consistent?

```text
Stable 20 years
     ↓
High Reputation
```

versus

```text
Changed 4 times this month
     ↓
Low Reputation
```

***

### Travel Outcomes

Did travelers arrive where expected?

```text
10 million departures
10 million arrivals

Excellent Reputation
```

versus

```text
10 million departures
9.8 million arrivals

Reputation Penalty
```

***

### Audit History

How often does inspection succeed?

```text
Inspection 1: Pass
Inspection 2: Pass
Inspection 3: Pass
```

Repeated successful audits increase trust.

***

### Link Continuity

In your live-link system:

```text
Link A
   ↓
Marker A
```

The longer that relationship remains unchanged, the higher the confidence.

A destination that frequently changes endpoints would become less trusted.

## Network Reputation

The idea can grow beyond individual markers.

Instead of:

```text
Trust Marker
```

the system begins to trust:

```text
Marker
Port
Region
Authority
Network
```

For example:

```text
Earth Authority Reputation: 99.9

Earth Port 01 Reputation: 99.8

Earth Transit Region Reputation: 99.7
```

A destination inherits trust from the systems around it.

## Reputation Graphs

Eventually you get a trust graph.

```text
Earth Port
    │
    ├── Marker
    ├── Regional Beacons
    ├── Space Traffic Stations
    ├── Authority Registries
    └── Audit Records
```

Each relationship contributes evidence.

The destination becomes trusted not merely because it claims to be Earth Port, but because thousands of independent observations agree that it has been Earth Port for years.

## Detecting Marker Switching

This is where reputation becomes powerful.

Suppose someone plays a "three-card monte" game:

```text
Yesterday

Earth → Marker A
Mars  → Marker B

Today

Earth → Marker B
Mars  → Marker A
```

Authentication might see:

```text
Valid Marker
Valid Marker
```

and approve both.

A reputation system sees:

```text
50 years of Earth history
suddenly attached to Marker B
```

and immediately raises suspicion.

The marker may be valid, but the *history doesn't fit*.

## Advanced Form

The most sophisticated version becomes predictive.

Instead of asking:

> "Did something change?"

it asks:

> "Is this destination behaving like the destination it claims to be?"

The system compares:

* historical traffic patterns,
* authority records,
* neighboring markers,
* environmental measurements,
* endpoint characteristics,
* traveler outcomes.

If "Earth Port" suddenly begins exhibiting characteristics associated with some other destination, the reputation engine flags it long before a human operator notices.

So in the architecture, reputation-based identity is essentially **destination memory**: the network remembers what a destination has historically been, and uses that history to detect subtle marker substitutions that ordinary identity checks could miss.
