For a destination-identity system, historical relationships are often the largest dataset. A destination may have only a few active links, but decades of audits, marker assignments, sponsorships, and anomaly records.

The key principle is:

> Store current state for speed and history for reconstruction.

## 1. Versioned Relationships

Instead of replacing a relationship, give it a validity period.

Example:

```text
Earth Port
    USES
Marker A417

Valid:
2020-01-01
to
2026-03-15
```

Then:

```text
Earth Port
    USES
Marker B923

Valid:
2026-03-15
to Present
```

Nothing is deleted.

To reconstruct the network at any point:

```text
"What did Earth Port use
on 2025-01-01?"
```

the system selects the relationship valid during that period.

***

## 2. Event Sourcing

Rather than storing every graph state, store only changes.

Example:

```text
2020:
Marker Assigned A417

2024:
Sponsor Added

2026:
Marker Reassigned B923
```

The current graph can always be recreated by replaying events.

Advantages:

```text
Storage Efficient
Complete History
Easy Auditing
```

***

## 3. Relationship Lineage Chains

For critical identities, keep lineage explicitly.

```text
Earth Port

Marker History:

A400
 ↓
A410
 ↓
A417
 ↓
B923
```

Investigators can quickly see:

* what changed,
* when,
* why,
* who approved it.

This is especially useful for marker-switch detection.

***

## 4. Delta Storage

Store only differences.

Instead of:

```text
Graph Snapshot #1
100M Relationships

Graph Snapshot #2
100M Relationships
```

store:

```text
Snapshot
+
Changes
```

Example:

```text
Added:
Link-17

Removed:
Link-12

Modified:
Marker Assignment
```

This dramatically reduces storage needs.

***

## 5. Time-Partitioned Archives

Historical data can be partitioned.

```text
2026 Archive
2025 Archive
2024 Archive
```

or

```text
Daily
Monthly
Yearly
```

Most investigations focus on recent periods.

This keeps old history accessible without slowing real-time operations.

***

## 6. Relationship Compression

Many relationships remain unchanged for years.

Instead of:

```text
Earth → Marker A417
recorded every day
```

store:

```text
Earth → Marker A417

Start:
2020

End:
2026
```

One record represents six years of stability.

***

## 7. History Summarization

Very old data can be rolled up.

Instead of:

```text
10 million audit events
```

store:

```text
2020-2025 Summary

Audits: 10,000
Pass Rate: 99.98%
Failures: 2
```

while retaining raw data in deep archives.

***

## 8. Graph Snapshots + Event Logs

A common scalable design is:

```text
Current Graph
+
Periodic Snapshots
+
Event Stream
```

For example:

```text
Monthly Snapshot
```

plus

```text
All Events Since Snapshot
```

To reconstruct history:

```text
Load Snapshot
Apply Events
```

This is much faster than replaying years of events.

***

## 9. Hot vs Cold Storage

Split history by access patterns.

### Hot Storage

Frequently accessed:

```text
Recent 90 Days
Active Incidents
Current Lineage
```

### Cold Storage

Rarely accessed:

```text
5-Year-Old Audits
Retired Destinations
Historical Traffic
```

This keeps the operational graph fast.

***

## 10. Historical Relationship Graph

For investigations, maintain a separate history graph.

Current graph:

```text
Earth Port
   ↓
Marker B923
```

Historical graph:

```text
Earth Port
   ↓
A400
   ↓
A410
   ↓
A417
   ↓
B923
```

The operational system uses the current graph.

Auditors and investigators use the history graph.

***

### A Mature Architecture

A scalable design might look like:

```text
Current-State Graph
        +
Relationship Lineage
        +
Event Stream
        +
Periodic Snapshots
        +
Archived Historical Graph
```

This gives you:

* fast real-time queries,
* complete auditability,
* efficient storage,
* reconstruction of any past state,
* support for reputation and anomaly analysis.

In the destination-identity model we've been describing, lineage and event sourcing are especially valuable because they make unauthorized marker switching difficult to hide. Even if an attacker alters the current graph, the historical chain shows exactly when a marker changed, which authorities approved it, and what relationships existed before and after the change.
