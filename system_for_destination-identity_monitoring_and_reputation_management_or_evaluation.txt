In the destination-identity system we've been describing, anomaly thresholds should generally be **risk-based**, **historically informed**, and **multi-level**, rather than fixed values.

## 1. Start with a Baseline

First, define what "normal" looks like.

Example:

```text
Earth Port 01

Marker Changes:
0 per year

Link Reassignments:
0 per year

Arrival Accuracy:
99.999%

Audit Failures:
0

Sponsor Changes:
1 every 5 years
```

An anomaly is measured relative to that baseline.

***

## 2. Use Multiple Severity Levels

Instead of:

```text
Normal
Anomalous
```

use:

```text
Normal
Warning
Concern
Critical
```

Example:

```text
Destination Identity Changes

0 changes/year     = Normal
1 change/year      = Warning
2-3 changes/year   = Concern
4+ changes/year    = Critical
```

This reduces false alarms.

***

## 3. Classify Changes by Impact

Not every event deserves the same threshold.

### High-Criticality Events

These might trigger an alert immediately:

```text
Marker identity change
Link destination change
Sponsor withdrawal
Identity mismatch
```

Threshold:

```text
1 occurrence = Alert
```

because the risk is inherently high.

***

### Medium-Criticality Events

Examples:

```text
Reputation decrease
Traffic shift
Minor routing variance
```

Threshold:

```text
Several occurrences
before escalation
```

***

## 4. Relative Thresholds

Different destinations may require different thresholds.

Low-volume location:

```text
100 arrivals/day
```

High-volume location:

```text
10,000,000 arrivals/day
```

A discrepancy of 10 arrivals means very different things in each case.

Thresholds should scale to activity.

***

## 5. Statistical Thresholds

The reputation engine could monitor deviations from historical norms.

Example:

```text
Normal:
0.1% routing variance

Observed:
5%
```

The system detects:

```text
50× normal variance
```

and raises an alert.

Here the threshold is based on deviation from history rather than an arbitrary number.

***

## 6. Composite Risk Thresholds

Many mature systems don't alert on a single signal.

Instead:

```text
Risk Score =
Identity Change
+
Sponsor Disagreement
+
Traffic Anomaly
+
Audit Findings
```

Example:

```text
Marker Changed      +50
No Sponsor Approval +30
Traffic Shift       +10

Total Risk = 90
```

Thresholds:

```text
0-30   Normal
31-60  Warning
61-80  Investigation
81-100 Critical
```

This reduces noise while still detecting meaningful events.

***

## 7. Reputation-Based Threshold Adjustment

Highly trusted destinations can be monitored differently from new destinations.

Example:

```text
Established Destination
History: 40 years
```

might tolerate small fluctuations without alerting.

Whereas:

```text
New Destination
History: 2 weeks
```

could operate under tighter monitoring until enough evidence accumulates.

***

## 8. Consensus Thresholds

For multi-authority sponsorship, thresholds can be based on agreement levels.

Example:

```text
5 Authorities

5/5 agree = Normal
4/5 agree = Warning
3/5 agree = Investigation
2/5 agree = Critical
```

This is especially useful for detecting unauthorized reassignment attempts.

***

## 9. Dynamic Thresholds

A sophisticated system automatically adjusts thresholds.

During a major change:

```text
New Marker Installation
```

the system temporarily expects:

```text
Higher audit activity
Higher traffic variation
More configuration changes
```

and relaxes some thresholds.

After stabilization:

```text
Thresholds tighten again
```

***

## Practical Rule

In your marker-switching scenario, I'd use a principle like:

```text
Identity Changes
    ↓
Very Low Threshold

Behavior Changes
    ↓
Moderate Threshold

Traffic Variations
    ↓
Higher Threshold
```

In other words, if the network's entire trust model depends on stable destination identity, then:

```text
Marker Change
Link Reassignment
Sponsor Change
Authority Disagreement
```

should often have thresholds of **one event equals immediate investigation**, whereas ordinary operational metrics can tolerate a larger range of variation before being classified as anomalous. This prevents a "three-card monte" destination switch from hiding inside a sea of routine operational fluctuations.
