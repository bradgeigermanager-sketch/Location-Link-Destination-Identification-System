In a destination-identity system you've been describing, audits would probably occur at **multiple frequencies simultaneously**, rather than on a single schedule.

## Continuous Audits

For a live-link system, some checks would run constantly.

```text
Every Second:
- Link identity
- Marker identity
- Endpoint consistency
- Network topology
```

These are automated integrity checks.

They answer:

> "Has anything changed unexpectedly since the last check?"

Continuous monitoring is the strongest defense against the "three-card monte" marker-switching scenario you've been exploring.

***

## Daily Audits

Automated daily reconciliation could verify:

```text
Expected Links
        vs
Actual Links
```

and

```text
Expected Markers
        vs
Observed Markers
```

These audits focus on detecting:

* unauthorized reassignments,
* missing destinations,
* new destinations,
* unexpected configuration changes.

***

## Weekly Audits

Weekly reviews could involve human oversight.

Inspectors review:

* approval records,
* sponsorship changes,
* reputation changes,
* link modifications,
* destination additions.

The question becomes:

> "Did any authorized changes look suspicious?"

***

## Monthly Audits

Monthly audits might examine entire regions.

Example:

```text
Earth Region
    ↓
All Ports
    ↓
All Links
    ↓
All Sponsors
```

This catches issues that individual location audits might miss.

***

## Quarterly Audits

A deeper verification process:

```text
Physical Inspection
+
Identity Verification
+
Historical Review
+
Authority Confirmation
```

Auditors physically visit markers (or their equivalent in your system) and verify that records match reality.

***

## Annual Audits

These would probably be the most comprehensive.

The oversight group reconstructs the entire network:

```text
Known Network
      ↓
Current Network
      ↓
Compare
```

Questions include:

* Do all destinations still exist?
* Are all sponsorships valid?
* Are all identities still accurate?
* Have any hidden routes appeared?
* Has any reputation been improperly transferred?

***

## Risk-Based Audits

The most sophisticated system wouldn't audit everything equally.

Low-risk destination:

```text
50 years stable
No incidents
```

might receive:

```text
Quarterly inspection
```

while a high-risk destination:

```text
Recent reassignment
Recent anomaly
New sponsorship
```

might receive:

```text
Daily review
Continuous scrutiny
```

***

## Event-Triggered Audits

Certain events could automatically trigger immediate investigation:

```text
Marker change
New destination
Sponsor withdrawal
Unexpected arrivals
Link reassignment
Identity mismatch
```

In a mature system, these are more important than the calendar schedule itself.

***

## A Likely Mature Model

A realistic governance structure for your hypothetical network might look like:

```text
Continuous:
  Automated integrity checks

Daily:
  Network reconciliation

Weekly:
  Human review

Monthly:
  Regional audits

Quarterly:
  Physical verification

Annually:
  Full-network audit

Immediately:
  Any suspicious event
```

The key idea is that the more critical the destination and the larger the consequences of misrouting, the more the system shifts from **periodic inspection** to **continuous verification**. In a network where marker switching could redirect travelers to entirely different locations, waiting months for an audit would likely be considered unacceptable; the system would need ongoing monitoring supplemented by increasingly deeper periodic reviews.
