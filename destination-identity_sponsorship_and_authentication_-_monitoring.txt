Continuing describing the destination-identity system we've been building, **authority sponsorship** is essentially a trust-transfer mechanism.

## Core Idea

A trusted authority says:

> "We have inspected this new destination and are willing to put our reputation behind it."

Instead of the destination proving decades of history it doesn't yet have, it temporarily borrows trust from an established authority.

```text
Transit Authority
      ↓
Sponsors
      ↓
New Destination
```

## Basic Sponsorship Model

Suppose a new spaceport is created.

```text
Port Name: Alpha Gateway

History: None
Reputation: None
```

The Earth Transit Authority reviews it:

* verifies the marker,
* verifies the destination identity,
* verifies the ownership records,
* verifies the infrastructure.

After approval:

```text
Sponsored By:
Earth Transit Authority

Status:
Provisionally Trusted
```

The destination receives an initial reputation based on its sponsor.

***

## Reputation Inheritance

A sponsored destination might inherit a portion of the sponsor's reputation.

For example:

```text
Earth Transit Authority
Reputation: 98
```

New destination:

```text
Base Reputation: 50

Authority Contribution:
+30

Initial Reputation:
80
```

The destination is trusted enough to operate but not trusted as highly as its sponsor.

***

## Risk to the Sponsor

Sponsorship is meaningful because it creates accountability.

If a sponsored destination repeatedly fails:

```text
Alpha Gateway
    ↓
Routing Anomalies
    ↓
Security Incidents
```

the sponsoring authority also suffers reputation damage.

```text
Destination Reputation: -20

Sponsor Reputation: -3
```

This gives authorities an incentive to sponsor carefully.

***

## Multi-Authority Sponsorship

A more advanced system might require multiple sponsors.

```text
Transit Authority
        +
Security Authority
        +
Regional Authority
```

Together they approve a destination.

```text
Alpha Gateway

Sponsored By:
Transport Authority
Security Authority
Port Authority
```

Trust becomes distributed.

***

## Sponsorship Tiers

Not all sponsorships are equal.

### Identity Sponsorship

Authority confirms:

```text
This marker is genuine.
```

***

### Operational Sponsorship

Authority confirms:

```text
This destination is safe to use.
```

***

### Security Sponsorship

Authority confirms:

```text
No signs of marker tampering.
```

***

### Routing Sponsorship

Authority confirms:

```text
This destination is correctly linked.
```

A destination may possess some sponsorships but not others.

***

## Conditional Sponsorship

New destinations might initially receive limited trust.

```text
Maximum Daily Arrivals:
100

Enhanced Monitoring:
Enabled

Audit Frequency:
Weekly
```

As positive history accumulates, restrictions are removed.

```text
3 months successful operation
        ↓
Expanded Approval

1 year successful operation
        ↓
Full Approval
```

***

## Sponsorship Chains

A sophisticated system could create trust chains.

```text
Galactic Transit Council
          ↓
Earth Transit Authority
          ↓
Earth Port Network
          ↓
Alpha Gateway
```

When evaluating Alpha Gateway, the network examines the entire chain.

This resembles how certificate authorities work on modern computer networks.

***

## Sponsorship and Marker Switching

In the marker-switching scenario you've been exploring, sponsorship becomes especially important.

Imagine:

```text
Earth Port
       ↓
Marker A
```

is suddenly replaced with:

```text
Earth Port
       ↓
Marker B
```

The system asks:

```text
Which authority approved Marker B?
```

If no sponsor exists:

```text
Status:
Unauthorized
```

If a sponsor exists:

```text
Who approved it?
When?
Why?
```

This creates a traceable chain of responsibility.

***

## Most Advanced Form

The most mature implementation would treat sponsorship as a living relationship rather than a one-time approval.

```text
Authority
    ↓
Continuous Monitoring
    ↓
Destination
    ↓
Periodic Audits
    ↓
Reputation Updates
```

In that model, authorities aren't merely creating trust—they are continuously maintaining it. If a destination's behavior changes, the authority must either reaffirm its sponsorship or withdraw it, causing the destination's reputation to drop until it can re-establish trust through new verification.
