In a destination-identity architecture, an **oversight group physically and logically verifying links at each location** is probably one of the most natural audit models;

The interesting part is that auditing can occur at several levels simultaneously.

## Level 1: Marker Audit

Inspectors visit a destination and verify:

```text
Location
    ↓
Marker
    ↓
Identity Records
```

Questions:

* Does the marker actually exist?
* Is it the correct marker?
* Does it match registry records?
* Has it been replaced?
* Have its attributes changed?

This is analogous to inspecting a lighthouse or airport beacon.

***

## Level 2: Link Audit

Instead of auditing only the marker, auditors verify all known links.

```text
Earth Port

Link A → Mars
Link B → Venus
Link C → Station X
```

The audit checks:

```text
Expected Destination
        vs
Observed Destination
```

This directly targets the marker-switching problem.

***

## Level 3: Reciprocal Verification

This is similar to the model you suggested.

Suppose:

```text
Earth → Mars
```

exists.

Auditors also verify:

```text
Mars → Earth
```

from the other side.

The network becomes self-checking.

```text
Location A confirms B
Location B confirms A
```

If one side reports a different relationship, an inconsistency appears.

***

## Level 4: Independent Oversight Teams

A stronger model avoids having the destination verify itself.

```text
Local Authority
Regional Authority
Independent Authority
```

Each performs separate inspections.

For example:

```text
Earth Port says:
Link → Mars

Regional Auditor says:
Link → Mars

Independent Auditor says:
Link → Station X
```

Immediately there's an investigation.

***

## Level 5: Network Reconciliation

The entire network is periodically reconstructed.

Imagine:

```text
Location A
Location B
Location C
Location D
```

Every location reports:

* its markers,
* its links,
* its destination identities.

The oversight authority builds a global map.

```text
Expected Network
        ↓
Observed Network
        ↓
Comparison
```

Unauthorized additions, removals, or switches become visible.

***

## Level 6: Link Lineage Auditing

One particularly powerful idea is maintaining a complete history.

```text
Earth Marker

2024 → Marker A
2025 → Marker A
2026 → Marker A
2027 → Marker B
```

Auditors ask:

> Why did Marker B appear?

Every transition must have a corresponding authorization trail.

```text
Who approved it?
When?
Under what authority?
```

***

## Level 7: Random Route Validation

Auditors occasionally perform test trips.

```text
Requested:
Earth Port

Expected:
Earth Port

Observed:
Earth Port
```

or

```text
Requested:
Earth Port

Observed:
Unknown Destination
```

This catches problems that registry checks might miss.

***

## Level 8: Distributed Witness Model

The most advanced version may not rely on one oversight group at all.

Instead:

```text
Location A audits B
Location B audits C
Location C audits D
Location D audits A
```

Combined with central authorities:

```text
Transit Authority
Security Authority
Audit Authority
```

you get a web of verification.

Every destination is continuously checked by multiple independent entities.

***

## Most Mature Audit Architecture

A sophisticated system would probably combine all of these:

```text
Physical Marker Inspection
+
Live Link Verification
+
Reciprocal Validation
+
Independent Auditors
+
Global Network Mapping
+
Historical Lineage Review
+
Random Route Testing
+
Multi-Authority Oversight
```

In such a system, a "three-card monte" marker switch becomes difficult because the attacker is not merely changing one link. They must also avoid detection by:

* local inspections,
* reciprocal destination checks,
* network reconciliation processes,
* historical audits,
* independent authorities,
* and test-route verification.

The audit process essentially turns the transportation network into a continuously verified graph, where every destination's identity is confirmed not only by itself, but by the destinations and authorities connected to it.
