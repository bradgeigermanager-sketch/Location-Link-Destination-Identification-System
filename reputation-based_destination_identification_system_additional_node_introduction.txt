In a reputation-based destination system, **new destinations have a bootstrapping problem**:

> They have no history, so how can they be trusted?

A sophisticated system would likely separate **identity confidence** from **reputation**.

```text
Identity = "Is this destination what it claims to be?"

Reputation = "Has this destination proven itself trustworthy?"
```

A newly created destination might have:

```text
Identity Confidence: 95
Reputation Score: N/A
Status: Probationary
```

even though it has no operational history yet.

## Stage 1: Sponsored Introduction

A new destination could inherit initial trust from established authorities.

```text
Earth Transit Authority
    ↓
Introduces
    ↓
New Port Alpha
```

The destination starts with a moderate reputation because a trusted authority is backing it.

```text
Authority Reputation = 98

New Destination Reputation = 75
```

rather than starting at zero.

***

## Stage 2: Observation Period

The system monitors early operation.

```text
Travel Events
Audit Passes
Identity Checks
Link Stability
```

Every successful verification increases confidence.

```text
Day 1: Score 75
Day 30: Score 82
Day 90: Score 91
```

***

## Stage 3: Trust Accumulation

The destination gradually builds a reputation through successful operation.

```text
100 successful arrivals
1,000 successful arrivals
10,000 successful arrivals
```

As evidence accumulates, historical continuity becomes more valuable.

A destination that has operated cleanly for years becomes harder to impersonate.

***

## Stage 4: Reputation Inheritance

A more advanced system could use a trust graph.

```text
Earth Authority
     ↓
Earth Port Network
     ↓
New Port Alpha
```

New destinations inherit some trust from connected entities.

Similar concepts exist in:

* certificate trust chains,
* professional accreditation,
* supplier qualification systems.

Trust flows through relationships.

***

## Stage 5: Reputation Decay Protection

A new destination should not be destroyed by lack of history alone.

Instead of:

```text
No History = Untrusted
```

the system treats it as:

```text
No History = Unknown
```

Unknown and untrusted are different categories.

```text
Trusted
Unknown
Suspicious
Compromised
```

A new destination begins as **unknown**, not **suspicious**.

***

## Stage 6: Confidence Intervals

An especially sophisticated system would track confidence separately from reputation.

For example:

```text
Destination A

Score: 92
Confidence: High
Evidence: 20 years
```

versus

```text
Destination B

Score: 92
Confidence: Low
Evidence: 2 weeks
```

Both have the same numerical score, but the first is supported by vastly more evidence.

This prevents attackers from creating a brand-new destination that immediately appears as trustworthy as an established one.

***

## Detecting Abuse

Without safeguards, an attacker could repeatedly create fresh destinations.

```text
Compromised Marker
      ↓
Create New Identity
      ↓
Reset Reputation
```

To prevent this, the system would likely retain history of:

* marker ownership,
* authority sponsorship,
* previous identities,
* link lineage.

Even if a destination receives a new name, investigators can trace its ancestry.

***

## The Most Mature Model

The most advanced version would treat reputation like a person's professional reputation:

```text
Identity
+
Sponsors
+
Operational History
+
Audit Results
+
Network Relationships
+
Time
=
Reputation
```

A new destination starts with little evidence but a valid identity. Over time, successful operation converts that initial identity into earned reputation. In a marker-switching scenario, this makes it difficult for someone to introduce a replacement marker and instantly grant it decades of accumulated trust—the new marker would have to earn that reputation or inherit it through tightly controlled authority processes.
